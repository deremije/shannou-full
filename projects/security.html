SECURITY DESKTOP APPLICATION

THE PROBLEM

Honeywell purchased a company which made desktop software for high-security contexts. They had historically been a provider of integrated security solutions to government, commercial and critical infrastructure customers throughout the world, but they wanted to expand their main product to other verticals. In order to be competitive in the marketplace, the software needed a major facelift, both for the outdated visual style and for modern interaction and usability improvements.

MY ROLE

I led a small team through the design process, from discovery and user research through interaction, visual design, prototyping and hand-off to development. I provided creative direction to a junior designer and also worked as an individual contributor.

DESIGN PROCESS

DISCOVERY & ANALYSIS

After conducting a deep dive workshop with the business, we learned about the product’s current functionality through product demos, interviews with stakeholders and technical support and delved into its intricacies by reading its technical and training manuals. We uncovered improvement quick wins by doing heuristic analyses and competitive reviews on the product.

PERSONAS & USER RESEARCH

Having first learned about the users only by talking with the business, we created a few personas which we needed to validate by talking to real people. We interviewed users at an Airforce base who used the software in order to fully understand the pain points of the product and how it fit into their current workflows. We performed contextual inquiries to uncover hidden needs and opportunites for new solutions that the product could provide.

IDEATION, BRAINSTORMING & PAPER PROTOTYPES

We collaborated on brainstorming ways to improve the product based on our findings. We sketched interaction ideas in low fidelity and iterated on them through rounds of guerilla testing in order to see how understandable our intial ideas were. Since the complicated legacy product required days of training to use, our goal was to simplify and make the UX straightforward enough that people with no experience would quickly pick up how it worked.

INTERACTION DESIGN AND HI-FIDELITY PROTOTYPES

We updated the information architecture based on the user's mental models and by the principal tasks they needed to accomplish. We created wireframes for some of the key user flows and then we applied the evolving Connected Building design language for the visual designs to them. Then, we created prototypes with the mock-ups to do usability testing with our principal users and to get their feedback. We did a few rounds of iterative design and testing to arrive at design solutions which were well accepted and understood.

ANALYSIS & OPPORTUNITY MATRIX

During our research, we collected key findings from each interview or contextual inquiry. We put these onto sticky notes and created affinity diagrams to make sense of the findings. We documented the opportunities for product features and solutions which could add value to the customers and a list of key changes that could be brought into the design and development roadmap. We then prioritized these in an opportunity matrix which plotted the difficulty to implement against the user impact in order to prioritize the next stages of the team's work.

DETAILED VISUAL AND PRODUCTION

We applied the quick wins and top priorities from the Opportunity Matrix into the rest of the screens we designed. We provided design interaction specs through high-fidelity prototypes and production specs through Inspect. We worked with the engineers on the team to ensure that our designs were feasible and did design QA to make sure that they were implemented correctly.
